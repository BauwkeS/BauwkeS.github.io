Tron - Battle Tanks

Remake of the arcade game 'Tron', but only the battle tanks portion. It is not fully accurate, but the old game has been mostly implemented using my own created C++ engine. This was a school assignment in my second year at Howest, DAE.

Tron - Battle Tanks
Game description:

The player must guide Tron's red battle tank through a maze and destroy all of the opposing blue enemy tanks by hitting each of them three times. The tank can warp to a random location in the maze by moving into a diamond in the center. In other levels, the enemy tanks are replaced by red Recognizers that are much faster and attempt to collide with the player instead of shooting at them.

Description:

We had to make an engine from scratch in C++20, using Visual Studio as IDE. After the engine was made, we had to create a game using the engine. The engine was created using mostly theory from the book "Game Programming Patterns" https://gameprogrammingpatterns.com and "Beautiful C++" as a helpful resource about good coding practices.

Technologies Used:
  • C++20
  • Visual Studio
Game patterns and features implemented:
  • Game Loop with Update method
  • Command pattern with pImpl
  • Observer & Event Queue
  • Component & Dirty Flag pattern
  • State pattern
  • Service Locator & Singleton pattern
  • Type Object pattern
  • Resource Management
  • Saving high scores in game to txt
Experience:

This was the most challenging task for me personally during my time in DAE. This project was done mostly independently with minimal guidance and feedback from school. I've learned a lot about C++ and core structures of a game engine and feel I've grown a lot during my spent on this. Looking back there are multiple things I would do differently now, but I am proud of what I accomplished with the knowledge I had at that time. The only regret I have, is leaving my level loader I created, just about not fully automated.

Geometric Algebra

During a math course in my second year at Howest, DAE, we had to create a small project using Geometric Algebra. We had the Flyfish library to help us with the basic implementations needed, but all the calculations used are a personal implementation of the concepts learned during the course.

Geometric Algebra
Motivation of the project from school:

"To improve the understanding of 3D PPGA operations and transformations, a practical C++ game project is provided to apply the concepts learned throughout the course. The focus lies on the application of rotations, translations, and reflections of objects to create interesting game mechanics. No traditional vector or matrix calculations should be used, all datatypes and operations should stem out of 3D PPGA." - De Pauw Ivo & Delombaerde Flor

Description:

Using PPGA formulas, I created a small game where you move around with a rectangle and try to catch as many points as possible. You are able to do this through using the continous movement of the rectangle and changing its direction, postion, through applying geometric algebra formulas.

Features implemented:

  • Movement/rotation using 3D motors (GEOA definition), where ’energy’ is stored in the third dimension
  • Speeding up / slowing down using said energy
  • Visualizing said energy
  • Collision/bouncing with the outside walls
  • Rotation around a pillar
  • Move selected pillar
  • Reflection about the pillar
  • By pressing a key, switching between multiple pillars in the game
  • Selecting a pillar with the mouse
  • Spawning new pillars in the game

Printing Party

Dive into the chaotic fun of managing your own money-printing factory in this PvP party game. Hire quirky workers, buy and upgrade crazy production lines and outsmart your friends with sneaky sabotage. Keep your factory buzzing and your coffers full to become the ultimate money-printing champion.

Hi! My name is Bauwke.

I am a game development student at HOWEST, where I'm in my last year of Digital Arts & Entertainment .

How did I get into programming?

Originally I got interested in game development after seeing VR and AR on the rise in 2018, it's something that still interests me to this day, though sadly I've yet to explore that technology. Most of all I'd like to do gameplay programming, as seeing my work actually change the game is what gives me the greatest satisfaction. I'm always open to learning new things, and I hope to never stop that process.

I enjoy working in existing engines since it allows me to do do what I enjoy most: Making things happen in the actual game.

Previous degree?

I also have another degree in Programming , where I decided to learn more about (mostly) application programming. It has given me a good foundation in basic programming languages and some more web development technologies, as well as soft skills needed in a company, like teamwork, reports, clear communication, and personal autonomy.

Other interests?

In my free time I like to play games (e.g. Honkai: Star Rail, The Long Dark, The Sims 4, Paladins...), and enjoy spending my time in my own dance group called Andromeda . Our dance group is run fully independently and it's helped me develop a strong sense of responsibility among other soft skills, such as planning, communication for events, and time scheduling. I am not alone in the dance group and we do most things as a team, but we each have our own responsibilities besides good teamwork. I have also picked up video and audio editing skills, and had the opportunity to performing on stage.

Over the years I've also earned a few degrees in flute and clarinet performance, which is currently on hold until I complete my studies at Howest. During all of this, I have definitely picked up a learning bug for new things and have never lost that. Once I am passionate about something, I do not quickly let it go.
